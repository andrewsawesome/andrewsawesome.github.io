Electronic Components

By understanding how electronic components work, it will be easier to find faulty components and replace them.

Capacitors

Capacitors are like rechargeable batteries with 2 differences.

  1. They tend to release their energy quicker
  2. They last many more “recharge cycles”

While capacitors are similar to batteries, they are NOT batteries.

There are 3 main types of capacitors:

  • Ceramic capacitors
  • Aluminum Electrolytic Capacitors(Electrolytic Capacitor)
  • Tantalum Electrolytic capacitors
  • Safety Capacitors(And other film capacitors)

    • Ceramic Capacitors

    Image source: EEPower

    Ceramic capacitors are the simplest and lowest-capacity capacitors. They use the simple methodology of capacitor design-two metals on either side of a dielectric. In the case of the ceramic capacitor, the dielectric is a ceramic disk. Being simple and small, these store a very low amount of electricity.

    Image source: EEPower

    Aluminum electrolytic capacitors hold much more capacity, but after about 20 years, it will start to leak and corrode. Electrolytic capacitors use tissue(dielectric) covered by foil(electrode). The capacitor is then soaked in electrolyte.

    You can tell the polarity of an electrolytic capacitor by the negative marking. On a through hole electrolytic capacitor, there is a stripe down the negative side. On Surface mount electrolytic capacitors, there is a semicircle on the negative side of the capacitor. You can also see the through hole capacitor codes below.

    Image source: EEPower

    Tantalum capacitors consist of anode and cathode foils, with a dielectric of paper impregnated with liquid electrolyte. This does not leak and corrode when it dies, but instead shorts and acts like a wire.

    Film capacitors are primarily used for safety, going across the live wire. They are made from two pieces of plastic film(dielectric) covered in metal foil/metal film(electrode). If you see an old one of these, you should replace them.

    Resistors

    Resistors limit the amount of current electronic devices get. See the resistor codes below for understanding how to read resistors.

    Through Hole Resistor Code Chart

    Color Codes

    Digits

    Black: 0

    Brown: 1

    Red: 2

    Orange: 3

    Yellow: 4

    Green: 5

    Blue: 6

    Violet(purple): 7

    Gray: 8

    White: 9

    Multiplier

    Black: *1 or 1(e.g.10*1=10 omhs)

    Brown: *10 or 2(e.g. 10*10=100 ohms)

    Red: *100 or 3(e.g. 10*100=1KO or 1,000 ohms)

    Orange: *1,000 or 4(e.g. 10*1,000=10 kilo ohms or 10,000 ohms)

    Yellow: *10,000 or 5(e.g. 10*10,000=100 kilo ohms or 100,000 ohms

    Green: *100,000 or 6(e.g. 10*100,000=1 mega ohm or 1,000,000 ohms

    Blue: *1,000,000 or 7(e.g. 10*1,000,000=10 mega ohms or 10,000,000 ohms)

    Violet(Purple): *10,000,000 or 8(e.g. 10*10,000,000=100 mega ohms or 100,000,000 ohms

    Gray: *100,000,000 or 9(e.g. 10*100,000,000=1 giga ohm or 1,000,000,000 ohms

    White: *1,000,000,000 or 10(e.g. 10*1,000,000,000=10 giga ohms or 10,000,000,000 ohms

    Gold: *0.1 or -1(e.g. 10*0.1=1 ohm)

    Silver: *0.01 or -2(e.g. 10*0.01=0.1 ohms

    For 3-band Resistors

    Band 1: Tens digit(e.g. the 1 in 15)

    Band 2: Ones digit(e.g. the 5 in 25)

    Band 3: The Multiplier(e.g. the 3 in 103)

    For 4-band Resistors

    Band 1: Tens digit(e.g. the 1 in 15)

    Band 2: Ones digit(e.g. the 5 in 25)

    Band 3: The Multiplier(e.g. the 3 in 103)

    Band 4:The tolerance (in percentage)

    For 5-band Resistors

    Band 1: Hundreds digit(e.g. the 1 in 195)

    Band 2: Tens digit(e.g. the 9 in 195)

    Band 3: Ones digit(e.g. the 5 in 195)

    Band 4: The Multiplier(e.g. the 3 in 103)

    Band 5: The tolerance(in percentage)
